I received my PhD from the Department of Political Science at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. <!-- Modeling spillovers -->
My primary agenda focuses on improving our tools for understanding hard-to-observe social and political phenomena along two research programs. The first research program focuses on analyzing interference effects in settings where the researcher observes units' placement in a network, but not how they connect to each other. I propose a model selection protocol that overcomes this difficulty. The key is to use a distance metric to identify alternative model specifications that follow from a theory, and then choose among them using a model selection technique of choice. I illustrate the usefulness of this approach in the context of supervised learning model selection by revisiting the findings of an experiment on the spillover effects of election observers on voter registration irregularities in Ghana.

<!-- Survey experiments and block rand -->
The second research program focuses on navigating the bias-variance trade-off in survey and field experiments. In a chapter with Christopher Grady (USAID) and Jim Kuklinski (Illinois) in *The SAGE Handbook of Research Methods in Political Science and International Relations*, we discuss the merits and challenges of increased complexity in the design of survey experiments. I bring the lessons of our chapter to a solo-authored piece under review that introduces new tools to assess the validity of findings in double list experiments. This variant of the list experiment promises more precise prevalence estimates but comes with under-explored questionnaire design complications. In a working paper with Jake Bowers (Illinois) and Christopher Grady, we discuss the circumstances under which researchers should prefer biased yet precise estimators to analyze experimental data, including applications to block-randomization, covariance adjustment, and M-estimation.
